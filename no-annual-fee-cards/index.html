Frequently Asked Questions

Are no annual fee cards worse than premium cards?

Only if you actually use the premium card perks. Most people pay $550 for the Chase Sapphire Reserve and never step foot in an airport lounge. For 90% of cardholders, no-fee cards deliver better value because you're not starting $95-$695 in the hole every January.

Can I get travel rewards without an annual fee?

Absolutely. The Wells Fargo Autograph, Bank of America Travel Rewards, and Capital One VentureOne all offer legitimate travel earning without annual fees. You won't get airport lounge access, but you'll save $95+ yearly.

Do no annual fee cards have worse rewards?

Not necessarily. The Citi Double Cash's 2% flat rate beats many cards with annual fees. The difference is premium cards offer perks (lounge access, credits) that some people value. If you don't use those perks, you're paying for nothing.

Should I pay an annual fee for better rewards?

Math it out. If a $95 annual fee card earns you $200+ more in rewards than a no-fee alternative, it's worth it. But most people overestimate how much they'll earn and end up subsidizing the bank's profit margins.

Bottom Line: No annual fee cards aren't "beginner" cards or consolation prizes. They're often the smartest financial choice for people who understand that paying $95 to earn rewards you could get for free is... not smart. Pick the card that matches your spending, actually use it, pay it off monthly, and keep that $95-$695 in your pocket where it belongs.
