Best Business Credit Cards 2025

Finally, cards that treat business expenses like actual business expenses

Business credit cards are personal cards with better tax deductions and the ability to pretend your Target run was "office supplies." But if you're actually running a business—freelancer, side hustle, LLC, or actual company—these cards offer legitimately better rewards, higher limits, and perks that don't make you feel like a peasant when you expense lunch with a client.

TL;DR: Which Card for Which Business

Freelancers/Side Hustlers: Chase Ink Business Unlimited (1.5% on everything, no fee)

Heavy spenders with expenses: Chase Ink Business Preferred (3x on $150K/year, 100K bonus)

Flex culture CEOs: Amex Business Platinum ($695 fee, lounges, status, bragging rights)

Simple cash back lovers: Capital One Spark Cash Plus (2% unlimited, $150 fee but worth it at scale)

The Cards Worth Carrying

BEST OVERALL

Chase Ink Business Preferred

💳 Fee: $95 🎁 Bonus: 100,000 points 💎 Earning: 3x on $150K

The Ink Preferred is Chase's love letter to small business owners who spend money but don't want to pay $695 for the privilege. Earn 3x on the first $150,000 spent annually on travel, shipping, internet/cable/phone, and advertising. That's $4,500 in rewards on top of the 100K point sign-up bonus. The $95 fee is basically a rounding error.

Perfect for: Consultants, agencies, anyone who spends $5K+/month on business expenses.

Skip if: You're a hobby business that spends $200/month on domain hosting and pretends it's an empire.

Business vs. Personal Cards: What's the Difference?

Besides letting you write off more lunches as "business meetings," business cards offer:

  • Higher credit limits: Banks assume businesses spend more (they're right)
  • Better tax tracking: Separate expenses = easier accounting = less tax season crying
  • Employee cards: Give your team cards without making them authorized users on your personal account
  • Purchase protection for business expenses: That $3,000 laptop is covered
  • Not reported to personal credit: Business cards don't count toward your 5/24 or affect personal utilization (usually)
  • Flex on clients: An Amex Platinum makes you look more successful than a Discover it

Do You Actually Need a Business Card?

Honest Answer: You Need One If...

  • You're a freelancer/consultant with actual clients (even one client counts)
  • You have a side hustle that generates revenue (Etsy, eBay, whatever)
  • You run a registered business (LLC, S-corp, sole prop)
  • You spend $500+/month on "business expenses" and want better rewards
  • You want to separate business spending from personal (smart move)

You DON'T need one if: You have zero business activity and just want to pretend your Costco run is a business expense. (Don't do this. The IRS has entered the chat.)

Pro Tips for Business Card Optimization

1. Stack Business + Personal Cards

Business cards don't count toward Chase's 5/24 rule. Get all the personal cards you want, THEN apply for business cards. Free money exploit.

2. Use Your Business Name (Even if It's Just You)

Sole proprietor? Use your name as the business name. Freelancer? That's a business. Sell stuff online? Business. You don't need an LLC to get approved.

3. Pair Chase Ink with Sapphire Cards

Earn points on Ink cards, transfer to Sapphire Preferred/Reserve, redeem at 1.25-1.5x value for travel. This is the business version of the Chase trifecta.

4. Track Everything (Obviously)

Business card = tax deductions. But only if you track expenses. Use accounting software (QuickBooks, FreshBooks, whatever) or your accountant will hate you.

The Bottom Line

If you have a legitimate business—freelance, side hustle, LLC, whatever—business cards offer better rewards, higher limits, and tax benefits that personal cards can't match. The Chase Ink Preferred is the best all-around card for most small businesses. The Amex Business Platinum is for flex culture. The Spark Cash Plus is for people who hate thinking.

Pick the card that matches your spending, actually use it for business expenses, and stop pretending your personal Uber Eats is a "client meeting."
